Digital Learning Platform Implementation Study

UX Research | Multi-site Classroom Fieldwork

Context

A large education initiative rolled out digital classroom tools across public schools, but usage was uneven and classroom integration was inconsistent. Stakeholders needed to understand the gap between platform expectations and real classroom use.

Methods

  • 30+ stakeholder interviews (teachers, administrators, NGO intermediaries)
  • 200+ hours of classroom observation and video-based interaction analysis
  • Workflow mapping of classroom teaching routines with shared devices
  • Qualitative coding and synthesis in NVivo/MAXQDA

Key Insight

The main issue was not simply “low adoption.” Teachers were actively adapting the platform to local constraints such as shared screens, timing, infrastructure failures, and mixed learning levels. The product and implementation model did not match the workflow reality.

Outcome

Findings helped reframe the problem from compliance to workflow fit, supporting more grounded implementation and training recommendations. The work also produced reusable research artifacts for communicating classroom friction points to non-research stakeholders.

Virtual Rituals and Remote Interaction Research

NSF-Funded Research | Platform-Mediated Behavior

Context

During COVID-19, communities moved social and ritual events to video platforms. The research goal was to understand how users preserved participation, meaning, and coordination when in-person interaction shifted to digital tools.

Methods

  • Interviews and focus groups with participants and organizers
  • Analysis of recorded virtual events to identify recurring coordination patterns
  • Cross-case comparison of interaction practices across event types
  • Research synthesis for mixed audiences (academic and public-facing)

Key Insight

Users were not just “using Zoom.” They developed new interaction norms to recreate social presence, visibility, and timing. Small platform affordances shaped participation more than expected, especially around turn-taking and shared attention.

Outcome

The work generated practical insight into mediated interaction and informed recommendations for designing and evaluating remote experiences that depend on social coordination, not just functional access.

Civic Engagement Participation Process Study

Community Systems Research | Service & Process Insight

Context

A community development initiative wanted broader public participation in local planning, but engagement remained uneven. Leaders needed evidence on where the process was excluding or discouraging participants.

Methods

  • Interviews, focus groups, and participant observation across community settings
  • Stakeholder mapping and communication pathway analysis
  • Qualitative synthesis of barriers to participation and trust
  • Translation of findings into process redesign recommendations

Key Insight

Participation barriers were not only informational. They were built into the structure of meetings, stakeholder roles, and communication expectations. The process itself created friction for many residents.

Outcome

Findings informed changes to outreach and meeting design, and helped stakeholders see participation as a service design problem rather than a simple communication problem.
